## Product Vision

Product vision is aspirational and concisely communicates where the product hopes to go and what it hopes to achieve
  in
  the long term. It serves as a guide and reminder to all stakeholders involved in the product’s development about the
  shared objective they are trying to achieve with the product.

The vision should be:
    * Customer focused
    * A desired end state
    * Aspirational
    * Memorable
    * Challenging to achieve

Developing a product vision requires research on your market, your customer, and the problem the customer has that you
are trying to solve.  When defining your product vision, be aware of how your product serves the product group and corporate vision these operate as guardrails to your product vision.  Consult with your product group/line leads and business technology partner to understand how your product fits within the overall vision.

Product vision is not a perfect fit for all products in Platform+ Tech. 

Your product vision is not your product strategy. Your product vision is between your company vision and your product strategy.

* Company vision helps guide your company
* Product vision helps guide your product
* Product strategy is the direction you will take to build your product.

### Product Vision Board

* The importance of establishing a Vision Board
* Provides focus and enables aspirational thinking
* Helps clarify direction of your product
    * Focus on what you want, leaving unhelpful thoughts behind
* Serves as inspiration for product teams
* Enables accountability through continued reminder of your goals

## Product Market Fit

*“Product-market fit means being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market.”*

*Marc Andreessen*

A major milestone for product-market fit is in defining what the MVP is, the minimum set of features needed to ship code
and get feedback.

Introducing an MVP offering to the market is the basis for enabling lean product delivery – the idea that everything we
deliver should drive customer feedback into development, shaping how we iterate on product over time to achieve market
fit.

### Determining if You Have Product-Market Fit

#### Generic Signs

You might see an increase in acquisition, retention, engagement, revenue, customer satisfaction, and advocacy for your
product if you have achieved a product-market fit.

#### The Happy Reference Customer

* Ideal for B2B
* A reference customer is a real customer who is running your product in production, who has paid real money, and is
  willing to tell others how much they love your product.
* Because your strategy should include focusing on and satisfying one target market at a time, ideally, you’d develop 6
  reference customers for one target market, then 6 for the next, and so on.

#### Sean Ellis Test

It’s simple a Survey to your users (those in your target market that have used the product recently, at least a few
times). You ask them :

How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?

* Very disappointed
* Someone disappointed
* Don’t care
* No longer relevant because I no longer use

If more than 40% of users would be very disappointed, you likely have product-market fit.
